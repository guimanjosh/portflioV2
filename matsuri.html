Applying Design Principles: Mapping

Last year's developer listed all of the previous team members' names in an unorderly fashion. Using the concept "mapping" from Design of Everyday Things, I reordered each name such that the row being analyzed from the picture aligns with the names from a row being read. In addition, I used CSS to color the text so it is easier to match the name and the face!

Creating an important flier design, distributing fliers, an accessible information website with good design, and utilizing social media websites are the most important factors in carrying out effective advertising for the UCSD Matsuri event. Using both traditional and modern advertising methods is important to optimize the audience reach in order and coverage. Moreover, carrying out traditional flyering was important to reach those who do not typically use social media or audiences who minimize using technology in general. Additionally, traditional flyering can also reach those who may have missed or skipped the online advertisement. On the other hand, the advertisement committee leveraged the fact that a large proportion of our target does use the internet and advertised online as well. Having an accessible website that provides a clear and concise itinerary for event attendees was important in encouraging them to commit to the event. Having an organized schedule of events, providing visual lists of Japanese foods being sold and games offered helped provide incentives for our audience to attend. The Matsuri data analysts noted that a large proportion of event goers from previous years attend primarily for the food. Music and dance performances were another important factor as they provide our audience rich auditory and visual entertainment. Lastly, the games helped gain younger attendees and those who enjoy games in general.

Final Product and Impact

"Josh, I love the look of the new website!! 😍" - Saya, Matsuri Coordinator

The final product I created for the Japanese student organizations as an advertisement committee was a new version of the event website. Redesigning and organizing information on the website was crucial in effective advertisement and providing incentives for people to attend. The website served as an important foundation for the event and was accessed by approximately 2,200 people in the greater San Diego area.

Through the use of flyering and social media advertisement posts, the advertisement committee was able to increase the event visibility and access to the website. Specifically, we found that around 800 people visited the website through facebook.

Although this was our first virtual event and its transition to online was rushed, roughly 120 people attended the festival for that year, setting the bar for virtual events.
